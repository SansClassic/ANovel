Chapter 7 - Regretful Actions

A week had passed since the assassination of Eleria’s king.

After Celestara’s swift takeover, the world fell eerily quiet. Too quiet.

Aina had been granted freedom to roam the conquered kingdom—but she didn’t take it for granted. The stares, the whispers, the silent fear that followed her like a shadow… it was suffocating. No one said a word to her. No one dared. But she could feel it in the air: they didn’t see her as human.

She didn’t want this.

Wait… want?

It was a strange realization—Aina wanted something. Something that wasn’t told. A choice. A thought that belonged to her.

And so she left the city behind, wandering past crumbling watchtowers and overgrown roads, back to the edge of the forest—back to the small mound of earth where a boy now rested beneath the soil.

The one who played the harmonica.

She knelt beside his grave, not sure why she came back. Her head ached with the weight of a thousand borrowed memories—grief, longing, laughter, pain—all tangled together in a storm that wasn't hers, yet lived inside her.

It hurt to remember.
But forgetting wasn’t an option either.

One by one, she unraveled the moments stored in her mind—fragmented memories not her own, yet deeply felt.

A father… picking up his newborn child.

She could feel it all—his trembling hands as he held the baby close, the warmth in his chest, the overflowing joy that clouded his every thought.

His plans for the future… a house filled with laughter, teaching the child to walk, to read, to dream.

A man sitting beside a bonfire, surrounded by friends he hadn’t seen in years—smiling, laughing, retelling old stories like no time had passed.

A man who wished to change the world through his actions—to build, to heal, to lead it somewhere brighter.

A man at his lowest, lost and broken… until fate gave him a soulmate. Someone who saw him—not the scars, not the past—just him.

All these memories.

All these sensations.

All these emotions.

Happiness. Sadness. Anger. Greed. Pain.

They weren’t hers. And yet… they felt familiar.

She longed for her own emotions—to feel something that was hers, raw and real.

To feel human.

But deep down… she knew better.

She wasn’t born. She was made.

A weapon—nothing more.

Aina turned away from the quiet grave, from the memories that weren’t hers, and made her way back to the kingdom of Eleria—now a fractured land under Celestara’s slow, steady occupation.

She wasn’t looking for peace or closure.

She was looking for him—the mysterious man behind her creation.

She didn’t know why… curiosity? Anger? Or maybe, just maybe, a desperate need to understand herself.

But before she could step past the castle gates—

A war cry erupted from the southern fields.

"CHARGE!!!"

Aina froze.

Soldiers.

Familiar armor. Faded banners.

The remnants of Eleria’s military—those who refused to surrender. Who still clung to the hope of rebuilding their fallen kingdom.

They came charging across the open hills like a final wave against the tide.

Her eyes narrowed.

She didn’t want this.

But she knew these people—they would bring chaos, and Celestara would respond with force. The last thing the world needed now was another bloodbath.

So she moved.

In a blink, Aina crossed the battlefield with inhuman speed, catching one of the soldiers mid-swing. Her hand clamped around the shaft of their spear.

Instinct told her to kill.

To silence the threat.

Her blade was already half-raised… before she stopped herself.

Her heart pounded—not from fear, but hesitation. Guilt. Humanity.

She didn’t want to kill. Not anymore.

She looked into the soldier’s eyes—young, scared, desperate.

“What future might you have had, if not for me?”
(I wish I didn’t have to wonder that)

With a breathless whisper, Aina dropped the blade.

Instead, she reeled back her arm and struck the man across the helmet. His iron visor bent inward from the force, and he collapsed instantly, unconscious but alive.

And then… came the rest.

Twenty-two soldiers, each charging with fury and grief.

But Aina held back.

No blades. No blood.

Only fists.

A blur through the battlefield—each strike a thunderclap, each soldier dropped with a single blow. Armor cracked, swords flew, but no lives were taken.

They fell one by one until silence returned to the field.

Aina stood in the middle of the field—surrounded by broken spears, unconscious soldiers, and a choice she never thought she’d make.

Minutes later, Celestara’s soldiers arrived, sweeping through the aftermath. Without a word, they moved past her to gather the unconscious rebels. No questions. No praise. Not even a glance.

As if she were a ghost.

Aina watched them work for a moment, then turned and walked away—toward the lab.

Toward him.

The lab was as cold and sterile as ever, humming faintly with arcane machines. And there he was—the mystery man—standing at his table with an all-too-familiar grin.

“You’re back! Did you have fun with your day off? I sure wish I had one! Haha!” His laugh echoed off the walls with an artificial cheer.

Aina said nothing, only narrowing her eyes at him.

He tilted his head slightly. “C’mon, you can talk to me, you know.”

A pause. Then, with a sigh, he leaned against the table and said, “You know... I used to have a daughter.”

Aina blinked.

“Her name was Dahlia,” he continued, his tone softening. “She was everything to me. Bright, curious, full of questions I couldn’t always answer. But this job… this cursed work...”

He shook his head.

“I was expected everywhere at once. I didn’t even have time to breathe. But one day—I had to go. A long expedition. Deep into what we now call The Dark Forest.”

Aina’s brow furrowed.

He smiled bitterly. “That forest is… a creature of its own. The paths shift like tides. The trees move, hunting sunlight. It’s a maze no map can hold.”

He glanced up at her.

“What does this have to do with Dahlia?”

He exhaled.

“She wanted to be an adventurer. Always said she’d explore every inch of the world, even the parts I warned her about. I told her I could provide for her forever, that she didn’t need to risk herself. But she said that would mean living without a dream.”

He paused, the silence pressing down.

“I let her go.”

Another breath.

“I shouldn’t have,” he whispered.

His fingers clenched tightly around the edge of the table.

“I should’ve known she’d go. I knew her… and still, I let her go.”
His voice trembled. “I begged the king. I begged him to send an expedition.”

Aina stayed silent, listening.

“...‘What benefit would it gain me if she were alive?’”

He chuckled bitterly—emptily.

“I stared at him, hoping... praying it was just a test. That maybe, just maybe, he'd change his mind.”

A long pause.

“But a day passed. Then a week. Then months. Nothing. No sign of her. No rescue. No grave.”
His eyes narrowed. “I couldn’t bear it. The thought that… if we had just sent someone—if I had insisted—she might still be alive.”

He turned away, looking at the flickering glyphs on the wall as though they might give him peace.

“So I lied. I asked the king for permission to enter the forest. Said it was for research—‘sample collection,’ I called it.”

A breath. Sharp. Shaky.

“I searched. Day after day. Week after week. Every path, every grove, every tree that had moved from where it was the day before.”
“I found nothing. Not even bones.”

He stopped, then slowly looked back at Aina.

“Until I discovered it.”

His voice dipped into something darker.

“I found a way out. A path that didn’t lead back to our lands.”

Aina tensed.

“It was… wrong. The air was thick. Rotten. Like the land itself was dying.”
“It wasn’t part of this world. It was another place entirely.”

“It’s what we now call The Demon Lair.”

He smiled faintly, but there was no joy.

“The true home of the monsters. The breeding ground of everything that bleeds into our world.”

He paused, then smiled—hollow and distant.

“You want to know what I did?”

Aina didn’t respond. She didn’t need to.

“Of course I went in,” he said, his tone low. “I was terrified. The air alone felt like it was burning through every living cell in my lungs. But I couldn’t turn back.”

“I kept walking—slow, careful steps. Avoiding any monster I could. But hours passed… maybe more. Time felt wrong there. And still, I couldn’t find her.”

His eyes clouded, drifting to the past.

“Then… in the distance, I saw it.”

His voice dropped, reverent and fearful.

“A castle. Massive. Towering. Its sheer size felt like it was pressing down on my soul.”

He stopped, a chill creeping into his breath.

“It felt… wrong. Alive, almost. Breathing. Watching.”

He shook the thought off.

“No matter. I approached it. Step by step. Took me hours just to reach the gates, the castle was that huge.”

“I expected monsters—crowds of them. Legions. But… no.”

His fingers trembled slightly.

“It was empty. Completely. Just silence. Too silent.”

“Inside, it stretched endlessly. Pillars—each wider than the trunks of ten trees—reached up into a ceiling I couldn’t even see. It was as if the roof was swallowed by the void itself.”

“Then… I saw him.”

The Demon King.

He swallowed, as if the words themselves were bitter.

“Every instinct in my body screamed at me to run. To kneel. To beg. I could feel it—one wrong move and I would die. Just like that.”

“But before I could even think of running... he spoke.”

“Interesting… A human… wandered so far from their safe little cage, and into my home?”

The man exhaled sharply.

“You’ve done well to survive the entire region of monsters out there,”
the Demon King’s voice echoed from the throne, each word laced with amusement.
“I can feel it. You’re nothing but a mere ant that somehow managed to cross a waterfall.”

His laughter echoed again—dark, hollow, almost playful.

“I can’t even sense an ounce of magic from you. Quite pathetic.”

A pause. A shift.

“But for you to have made it here at all… I’ll give you one chance. Turn back now. While I’m still being nice.”

Nice?
The Demon King… being nice?

The man clenched his fists, his voice shaking.

“I—I came here… I was searching for my daughter. She’s gone missing. I think she… she might’ve wandered into the Dark Forest.”

Silence.

Then, a low hum.

“Hmmm… You really went this far for your own flesh and blood?”
The Demon King’s tone turned mockingly soft.
“How touching. How quaint.”

He leaned forward from the throne, just enough for his crimson eyes to shine through the gloom.

“Humans and their attachments. Always chasing love. Purpose. Redemption. It’s adorable, really.”

His voice deepened, almost disappointed.

“But let me ask you something, father of the year… What would you sacrifice for her return? Your freedom? Your body? Your soul?”

The man’s breath caught.

“My soul means nothing if my daughter can be back in my arms,” he said firmly, his voice steel.

Silence. As though the Demon King were holding in laughter.

The Demon King gave an ominous smile.

“Then, how about I tell you a way?...”

End of Chapter
