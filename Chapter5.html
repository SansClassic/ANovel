Chapter 5 — Morality: Nothing More Than Words

Aina stepped out of the bar and into BrightVale.

She needed to grab something from her personal lab before heading to the meeting.

Once, this village had been bright. Warm. Alive.

Now it was a graveyard with roofs.

People were buried beside their own homes—makeshift graves marked by broken tools or worn shoes. Grief didn’t even sting anymore. What’s the point of mourning when death is just a few minutes away?

The town was quiet. Suffocatingly so.

Some villagers were lying down, trying to sleep off the hunger. Children—barely able to walk—still tried to play with rocks and sticks, smiling as though pretending could fill their bellies.

Then the village chief spotted her.

“Hey, Aina. Going out again?” He coughed. “Welp... I do hope you’re doing better than the rest of us.”

He tried to flash a smile, but it faltered halfway. “You’ve done everything you could. I know that. You didn’t sleep again last night, did you?”

Aina gave a tired shrug. “Yeah… not like I could sleep. I kept thinking, kept... trying to find something to fix this mess.”

The chief’s brows knit. “How long’s it been now?”

Aina rubbed her eyes. “Eh… a week maybe? Heh.”

She forced a small smile. It looked more like a wince.

The chief’s smile vanished.

“…A week of no sleep? Aina…”

His voice softened—genuine, heavy with concern.

“Can’t sleep, Chief. You know how it is…”

She kept walking.

Aina arrived at her home.

The door creaked softly open—and inside, warmth greeted her.

“Aina! You’re back!” Rein’s voice was bright as ever. “So how’d the village look? Is everyone okay?”

Aina, still on edge from the walk through BrightVale, felt her guard ease just a little.

“Well... today’s checkup is still terrible,” she muttered. “But hey—could be worse.”

Rein gave a hopeful nod. “Yeah, I get that. Oh! Lydia said I could stay home today! Weird, right? Considering the situation out there…”

“I mean, the royal guard’s gotta be needed more than ever!”

Aina’s brow furrowed. “She did…? Did she say why?”

Rein shrugged, still cheerful.

“Said I probably needed rest after working a whole month straight. Which—okay, fair. I’m exhausted. But still! I can’t sit still knowing people out there need help.”

He smiled wide, almost proud of his guilt.

Aina forced a chuckle. “That would be awesome... but maybe she’s right. You should rest. Just for today.”

She moved toward the kitchen. “Your ration’s in the fridge, by the way.”

“Okay! Thanks, Sis!”

Rein vanished into his room. Aina stood still, alone with her thoughts.

("...Something’s off.")

She stared at the closed door.

(""Rein would never choose rest. Not even if he collapsed. He’d jump into fire if it meant saving a soul...")

Her thoughts narrowed like a blade. ("Lydia did something.")

The weight of that idea settled over her shoulders—but she shook it off, exhaling through her nose.

("Whatever. What’s the worst that could happen…?")

Aina turned away and made her way to the back room—her lab.

She opened the heavy door with a gentle push. The scent of dust and scorched metal greeted her like an old memory.

Inside, the space was chaos.

Three months of nonstop experimentation had turned the lab into a graveyard of ideas—scattered metal fragments, cracked magic crystals, wiring twisted around rusted pipes, even a few charred demon bones lined the workbench like trophies of failure.

Amidst the mess, she grabbed a roll of schematics—blueprints of the Foundation’s internal structure. Annotated, creased, stained with ink and ash. It was her lifeline, the one map she trusted to guide her toward a replacement for the lost core.

But even now... it was still incomplete.

No matter what she tried, nothing had clicked. Nothing fit the way it needed to.

With a tired sigh, she rolled up the schematics, tucking them beneath her arm, and headed toward the front door.

But before she could reach for the handle—

“Wait!” Rein stepped into view.

“You’re going out again? To where?” His tone was curious, but concern lingered beneath it.

Aina glanced back at him and offered a casual smile.

“Ah, Lydia called me in for a meeting. Should be quick.”

Rein gave a small nod, but the unease didn’t leave his face. He fished something from his pocket—a worn phone—and dangled it toward her.

“Alright. If anything happens, call me, okay?”

Aina gave him a soft chuckle. “Yeah, of course, Rein.”

He waved. “See you tonight, then!”

“Yeah… see you tonight.” Aina replied, voice just a little quieter than before.

She stepped out the door, the familiar chill of BrightVale air brushing her cheeks as she descended the worn path.

The wind had picked up.

The skies were dark.

But her footsteps carried her forward—toward the meeting, and whatever truth waited on the other side.

Aina stepped into the meeting chamber.

Lydia stood at the far end, facing the wall. Her cloak hung heavy, her posture distant—like she was trying to disappear into the stone itself.

“Hey, Lydia,” she called, holding documents. “Got the reports ready for the week…”

Silence.

“Uh… Lydia?”

Still no answer.

Aina was just about to crack a joke—something dumb to break the tension—when Lydia finally spoke.

Her voice was low. Strained.

“...Sorry, Aina.”

She didn’t turn.

“Maybe… morality is no longer a choice.”

And then—

“Guards. Pin her.”

Before Aina could react, two armored guards burst from the shadows behind her. Rough hands grabbed her arms and shoulders, slamming her to the cold stone floor.

The impact knocked the breath from her chest.

Her mind went blank.

(This… is really happening? No...)

“H-Hey… Lydia?” she wheezed, struggling against the weight pinning her down. “W-What’s going on? D-Did I do something wrong? Haha… y-you know I’d never do anything bad, r-right?”

Her voice cracked.

Lydia still hadn’t turned. But now, slowly, she did.

Her face was haggard. Pale. Eyes rimmed with dark circles—eyes that had seen too much, lost too much, and carried too much.

She looked… broken.

“Aina,” Lydia said quietly, “Please… know this was never supposed to happen.”

She stepped forward, slowly. Every step felt like a weight dragging her down.

“Your sacrifice… I’ll make sure it won’t be in vain.”

“No—wait!” Aina cried out, her voice rising with panic. “Lydia, please! Give me another week—just a week! I swear I’ll find something! I’m close, I can feel it, just—just don’t do this!”

Lydia didn’t reply.

Her gaze was blank. Hollow. Distant.

“What about my brother…?”

Lydia flinched.

Her eyes wavered. For just a moment, guilt flooded her face—raw, unguarded. Her lips parted as if to say something, as if a part of her wanted to stop.

But then—

A breath. A blink. Her expression hardened again.

The mask returned.

in that split moment, Aina saw the hesitation.

“Who’s gonna take care of him? He still thinks this world has hope. Lydia—please! You can’t—”

The chamber door creaked. Footsteps. A figure entered, lab coat flaring slightly at the edges. Amelia’s face was unreadable.

She carried the device in her hands—cold, sleek, and unmistakably cruel in its design. Sharp, jagged clamps curved forward like the talons of a steel predator.

It was built for one thing only: To extract the heart.

“Here,” Amelia said softly, her voice barely above a whisper. She didn’t look at Aina. “I made it… as fast as I could.”

She held it out.

Lydia hesitated—just for a moment—then took the device with steady hands.

Aina’s breath hitched, her chest tightening like it was already being crushed.

“Wait…” she choked out, voice trembling. “No. No—please.”

Lydia’s eyes hardened.

“Pick her up,” she commanded. “H-Hold her. Don’t let her move.”

The guards obeyed.

Aina was forced down to her knees, her arms locked behind her, head yanked upright.

The cold floor bit into her legs.

Her heart pounded.

Her breath came sharp—suffocating.

Lydia stepped forward And Raised The Device

Her hand twitched. She hesitated But still, it moved toward Aina’s chest.

And then—

Aina gathered what little magic she had left—

And unleashed it A shockwave from her body—wild, uncontrolled, desperate.

The guards were flung back in a crash of metal. Lydia staggered. The device slipped from her grasp and clattered across the floor.

Aina gasped stood—but legs failed. The magic had drained her dry. Her vision swam. Still—

She refused to fall.

Aina stood, panting, as Lydia straightened across the room.

“I see...” Lydia muttered. “So you had a bit of fight left in you after all.”

A golden light pulsed in her palm.

A sword began to form—radiant and sharp, shaped from the golden light.

“I didn’t want this, Aina,” Lydia said quietly. “But I guess you’ve decided to make it harder than it needed to be.”

She struck. Aina dodged by instinct. Her body moved on memory, not strength. She slipped beneath the first swing..

The blade whistled past her cheek.

Another strike came. Aina staggered back, her feet scraping against the floor. She was cornered.

“You’re quick,” Lydia said, frustrated. “But what’s the point, Aina? You’re not getting out of here.”

Another strike. Aina staggered. Cornered.

Aina felt lightheaded. Her legs dragged. Her vision swam.

Aina’s eyes met Lydia’s one last time. She saw hesitation there—

she thought.For a moment, she believed it might stop.

Crack.

The blade caught the edge of her temple.

A sickening sound echoed through the chamber.

Aina collapsed.

Blood pooled where she fell, silver strands soaked with red.

Lydia froze.

She stared at what she had done—

Face pale. Hands trembling.

(" Oh... what have I... ")

But the shock didn’t last.

Her expression hardened. Cold. Detached.

As if she had already buried the pain.

She stepped forward grabbing the device. The device still hummed softly.

And lowered the device Into Aina's Chest.

End of Chapter
